# The 50/30/20 Budgeting Rule

## What It Is
The 50/30/20 rule divides after-tax (take-home) income into three categories:
- **50% Needs** — essential expenses you must pay
- **30% Wants** — discretionary lifestyle spending
- **20% Savings/Debt** — future financial security

## Calculating Each Bucket

### Take-Home Income First
Always calculate from **net income** (after taxes and mandatory deductions), not gross salary.

**Example at $5,000/month take-home:**
| Category | % | Monthly Budget |
|---|---|---|
| Needs | 50% | $2,500 |
| Wants | 30% | $1,500 |
| Savings / Debt | 20% | $1,000 |

**Example at $3,500/month take-home:**
| Category | % | Monthly Budget |
|---|---|---|
| Needs | 50% | $1,750 |
| Wants | 30% | $1,050 |
| Savings / Debt | 20% | $700 |

## What Counts as Needs (50%)
- Rent or mortgage payments
- Utility bills (electricity, gas, water, internet)
- Groceries (not restaurant meals)
- Health insurance premiums
- Minimum debt payments (credit cards, student loans, auto)
- Transportation costs to commute (fuel, transit pass)
- Childcare and basic clothing

**If your needs exceed 50%:** Reduce by renegotiating rent, refinancing loans, or cutting utilities before touching the 30% bucket.

## What Counts as Wants (30%)
- Dining out, takeaway, coffee shops
- Streaming subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify)
- Gym memberships
- Vacations and travel
- Entertainment (concerts, movies, games)
- Upgrades beyond basic clothing

## What Counts as Savings/Debt (20%)
- Emergency fund contributions (until 3-6 months funded)
- Retirement contributions above employer match (401k, IRA, Roth IRA)
- Extra debt payments above minimums (accelerating payoff)
- Investing in a taxable brokerage
- Saving for a house down payment or car

**Priority order for the 20%:**
1. Capture full employer 401k match (free money — always first)
2. Emergency fund to 1-month level
3. High-interest debt payoff (>7% interest rate)
4. Emergency fund to 3-6 months
5. Max Roth IRA ($7,000 limit in 2025)
6. Max 401k ($23,500 limit in 2025)
7. Taxable brokerage or other goals

## Adjusting for Your Situation

### High Cost-of-Living Cities
A 60/20/20 split may be more realistic when housing alone consumes more than 30% of income.

### Aggressive Debt Payoff Phase
Consider 50/20/30 (swap wants and savings) if carrying high-interest consumer debt.

### Building Wealth Phase (post-emergency-fund)
40/20/40 can accelerate wealth building once basics are secure.

## Why It Works
The rule forces explicit allocation decisions before money is spent. Without a framework, income tends to fill lifestyle spending first (lifestyle inflation) and savings become whatever is left — often nothing.
