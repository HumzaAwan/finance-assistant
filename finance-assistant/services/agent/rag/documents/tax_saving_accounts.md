# Tax-Advantaged Accounts: 401k, IRA, and HSA

## Why Tax-Advantaged Accounts Matter
Tax-advantaged accounts reduce your taxable income today (traditional) or future withdrawals (Roth). The difference in long-term wealth is significant: on a $500/month contribution over 30 years at 7%, avoiding even 22% annual tax adds roughly $150,000 to the end balance.

## 401(k) — Employer-Sponsored Retirement

### 2025 Contribution Limits
- **Employee limit:** $23,500/year
- **Catch-up (age 50+):** +$7,500 = $31,000/year
- **Total including employer contributions:** $70,000/year

### Traditional 401(k) vs Roth 401(k)
| | Traditional 401(k) | Roth 401(k) |
|---|---|---|
| Contribution | Pre-tax (reduces taxable income now) | After-tax (no deduction now) |
| Growth | Tax-deferred | Tax-free |
| Withdrawals | Taxed as ordinary income | Tax-free in retirement |
| Best for | High earners expecting lower taxes in retirement | Younger earners expecting higher taxes in retirement |

### The Employer Match — Always Capture It First
If your employer matches 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary, and you earn $60,000:
- 6% contribution = $3,600/year from you
- Employer adds $1,800/year (50% match)
- **That is an instant 50% return** — no investment beats this

**Rule:** Contribute enough to capture 100% of employer match before paying off any debt except very high-rate (>10%) credit cards.

## IRA — Individual Retirement Account

### 2025 Contribution Limits
- **Annual limit:** $7,000/year
- **Catch-up (age 50+):** +$1,000 = $8,000/year

### Traditional IRA vs Roth IRA
| | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA |
|---|---|---|
| Contribution | May be deductible (depends on income + workplace plan) | After-tax, never deductible |
| Income limit to contribute | None | Phase-out: $146k–$161k (single), $230k–$240k (married) in 2025 |
| Withdrawal in retirement | Taxed | Tax-free |
| Early withdrawal (before 59½) | 10% penalty + taxes | Contributions anytime free; earnings taxed + penalized |
| Required Minimum Distributions | Yes, starting age 73 | No RMDs during owner's lifetime |

### Backdoor Roth IRA
High earners above the Roth income limit can: contribute to a traditional IRA (non-deductible) → convert to Roth immediately. No income limit applies to conversions.

## HSA — Health Savings Account

### 2025 Contribution Limits
- **Individual:** $4,300/year
- **Family:** $8,550/year
- **Catch-up (age 55+):** +$1,000

### The Triple Tax Advantage
1. **Contributions are pre-tax** (reduce taxable income)
2. **Growth is tax-free** (invest in index funds inside the HSA)
3. **Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free**

No other account has all three advantages.

### Eligibility Requirement
Must be enrolled in a **High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)** — 2025 minimum deductible: $1,650 (individual), $3,300 (family).

### Optimal Strategy
Pay medical expenses out-of-pocket, save receipts, and let HSA investments grow. Withdraw tax-free decades later for any medical expense (including premiums after 65).

## Priority Order for Contributions
1. 401(k) up to employer match (free money)
2. HSA if eligible (triple tax advantage)
3. Roth IRA to max ($7,000)
4. 401(k) to max ($23,500)
5. Taxable brokerage (no limits, but no tax advantages)
