# UK Credit Scores — Agencies, Factors, and Improvement

**Topics:** uk_credit_scores, credit_score_factors, financial_literacy

Unlike the US (which uses a single FICO score), the UK has three separate credit reference agencies (CRAs), each with their own scoring model. Lenders choose which CRA(s) to query and set their own internal thresholds.

## The Three UK Credit Reference Agencies

### Experian

- **Score range:** 0 – 999
- **Bands:** Poor (0–560), Fair (561–720), Good (721–880), Very Good (881–960), Excellent (961–999)
- **Free access:** Experian free credit score (monthly update); full report via CreditExpert (paid) or Experian App
- **Used by:** HSBC, Barclays, Santander, Lloyds, John Lewis Finance, and many others

### Equifax

- **Score range:** 0 – 1,000
- **Bands:** Very Poor (0–438), Poor (439–530), Fair (531–670), Good (671–810), Very Good (811–1,000)
- **Free access:** ClearScore (free, weekly updates from Equifax data)
- **Used by:** Nationwide, Capital One, Vanquis, Argos Financial Services, and others

### TransUnion (formerly Callcredit)

- **Score range:** 0 – 710
- **Bands:** Very Poor (0–550), Poor (551–565), Fair (566–603), Good (604–627), Excellent (628–710)
- **Free access:** Credit Karma (free, real-time updates)
- **Used by:** MBNA, Tesco Bank, Ulster Bank, and others

## Key Factors Affecting Your Score

All three agencies weight similar factors, though exact algorithms differ.

### 1. Payment History (Most Important — ~35%)

- Whether you make payments on time, every time.
- Late payments, missed payments, defaults, and CCJs (County Court Judgements) severely damage scores.
- Even a single missed payment can remain on your file for **6 years**.
- Setting up direct debits for at least the minimum payment protects payment history.

### 2. Credit Utilisation (~30%)

- How much of your available revolving credit you are using.
- Calculated as: (total credit card balances / total credit limits) × 100.
- **Target: below 30%.** Below 10% is ideal for maximising your score.
- High utilisation (above 50%) signals financial stress and hurts scores.
- Spreading debt across multiple cards at low utilisation is better than maxing one card.

### 3. Length of Credit History (~15%)

- How long your oldest account has been open.
- How long you have held each type of credit.
- Closing old accounts (especially your oldest card) reduces average account age — avoid unless necessary.

### 4. Credit Applications and Hard Searches (~10%)

- Every time a lender runs a full credit check, a hard search appears on your file.
- Multiple hard searches in a short period suggest financial difficulty — each one can reduce your score by a few points.
- **Soft searches** (used for pre-approval or quote tools) do **not** affect your score.
- Space out applications by at least 3–6 months if possible.
- Mortgage comparison within a 14-day window is typically treated as one search.

### 5. Credit Mix (~10%)

- Having a mix of credit types (mortgage, credit card, personal loan, overdraft) can help.
- Not a reason to take on unnecessary debt — only a small factor.

## How Transaction Behaviour Affects Your Score

Your bank account transactions can indirectly affect your credit score through:

### Overdraft Use

- Regularly using an arranged overdraft or exceeding it is visible on your credit file.
- Persistent overdraft use (every month) flags financial stress to lenders.
- An unarranged (unauthorised) overdraft shows as a missed payment or default.

### Direct Debit Failures

- A returned direct debit (insufficient funds) is recorded on your file and reported to CRAs.
- One or two failed DDs in isolation are less damaging than a pattern; the bank may also close your account.

### Payday Loans and BNPL

- Use of payday lenders (Wonga, QuickQuid) is visible to lenders and signals cash flow problems.
- Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL) providers (Klarna, Clearpay) are increasingly reporting to CRAs — missed BNPL payments now affect scores.

### Regular Income (Positive Signal)

- Regular salary credits do not directly build your credit score but evidence financial stability when lenders do manual underwriting.

## Fastest Actions to Improve Your Score

| Action | Timeframe | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Register on the electoral roll | 1–3 months | Medium |
| Set up direct debits for all bills | Immediate (effect over 3+ months) | High |
| Reduce credit card utilisation below 30% | 1–2 months | High |
| Dispute incorrect information with CRA | 1–4 weeks | High (if errors found) |
| Avoid new credit applications | Ongoing | Medium |
| Close no accounts (especially oldest) | Ongoing | Medium |
| Use a credit builder card (low limit, paid in full monthly) | 6–12 months | Medium |

## Checking Your Credit Reports for Free

You are legally entitled to one free statutory credit report from each CRA per year. You can also access them free continuously via:

- **Experian App** — free basic score, monthly update
- **ClearScore** — free Equifax-based score, weekly update
- **Credit Karma** — free TransUnion score, real-time update

Check all three — errors on one bureau's file do not automatically appear on the others.

## Correcting Errors

If you find an incorrect entry (wrong address, account you do not recognise, payment marked late when it was on time):

1. Contact the lender directly — they can update the CRA on your behalf.
2. Raise a dispute directly with the CRA online.
3. Add a Notice of Correction — a 200-word statement explaining unusual circumstances (lenders must consider it).
4. If unresolved, escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) or ICO.
